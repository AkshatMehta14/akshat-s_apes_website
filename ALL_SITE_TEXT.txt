6 — REAL-WORLD EXAMPLES
================================================================================

Section title:

  Examples from the real world

Section intro:

  Even more impactful than indidual choices are governmental policies,
  regulations, and laws. These three examples/cards shows how a country or group
  of people can change their carbon footprint and emission curve by creating
  sustainable plans and really following them.

--- Example A — title:

  Denmark and wind electricity

--- Example A — body (bold "half" on site):

  Denmark has built wind capacity becuase of the oil shocks of the 1970s. Today
  this wind infustructure creates about <strong>half</strong> of their
  electricity. That didn't happen in one night and took years of planning and
  implementation. It took port upgrades, undersea cables, community buy-in, and
  a lot of engineering to keep the lights on when the breeze drops. Simply put
  countries can create sustainable plans for renewable energy and stick to
  them. It is a matter of how consistent and committed you are.

--- Example B — title:

  Costa Rica, renewables, and biodiversity

--- Example B — body:

  Costa Rica famously runs much of its grid on <strong>roughly 98%
  renewable</strong> electricity for a long time, leaning on hydro,
  geothermal, wind, and solar. Hydro has cons during droughts so its not like
  their solutions are completely perfect and foolproof. However, pairing a
  cleaner grid with aggressive forest protection matters for carbon stored in
  biomass and for species that need intact habitat.

--- Example C — title:

  California and vehicle emissions

--- Example C — body:

  California uses <strong>strict tailpipe and fuel rules</strong> for decades
  under federal waiver authority. Because California’s market is huge,
  automakers often build to that bar for the whole country. Cleaner cars cut
  local air pollution (ozone precursors, particulates) and, over time, CO₂ per
  mile as efficiency improves and electrics grow. For class, tie this to Unit 7
  chemistry in the troposphere, not only to climate graphs.

7 — EARTH SYSTEM INTERACTIONS
================================================================================

Section title:

  Earth system ands their interactions

Section intro:

  Lets start with the amosphere and ocean. They trade energy and chemicals all the time, open the pannels bellow to read more about how this occurs and what it looks like.

Callout (Unit 1):

  <strong>Unit 1: Earth systems.</strong> Matter and energy move between the
  atmosphere, hydrosphere, geosphere, and biosphere. Dependent on the climate, this movement speedsup, slows down, reverses etc. 

--- Panel 1 — accordion button title:

  Atmosphere and ocean

--- Panel 1 — main paragraph:

  The ocean is a giant buffer. Think of it as a large sponge that accumulates the heat and carbon emissions that we produce or are on the planet. 

--- Panel 1 — bullet 1:

  <strong>Ocean warming have the potential to move crucial fisheries that people depdend on and lead to massive algea blooms that can disrupt ecosystems. This currents are not stable. 

--- Panel 1 — bullet 2:

  When CO₂ dissolves, seawater becomes more acidic (lower pH). That makes it
  harder for some shell builders to form calcium structures.

--- Panel 1 — bullet 3:

  Warmer water and a bunch of other stressers can cause coral reefs to bleech and lose the algea that helps them survice. Once again this can also collapse ecosystems. 

--- Panel 2 — accordion button title:

  Climate and biosphere

--- Panel 2 — main paragraph:

  Spieces and animals all have a zone of compfort for their living conditions like temprature and moisture. Climate change can disrupt the living conditions of animals faster than they can adapt which kills them off. 

--- Panel 2 — bullet 1:

  <strong>Range shifts</strong> show up when species move poleward, uphill, or
  deeper to stay in tolerable conditions. Beucase plants and animals do not all move in the same ways or speed curical dynamics like predator or prey relationships fall out of ballance. 

--- Panel 2 — bullet 2:

  <strong>Extinction risk</strong> increases a lot for spieces that are specialists because they reliey on certain resources that may be lost from an enviornm,ent. 

--- Panel 2 — bullet 3:

  Phenology shifts too: earlier springs, mismatched migrations, and timing bugs
  that ripple through food webs.

--- Panel 3 — accordion button title:

  Climate and soil

--- Panel 3 — main paragraph:

  Soil is alive and easy to damage. When rainfall gets weirder (long dry spells,
  then violent storms), soil moisture swings stress plants and microbes that
  hold the ground together.

--- Panel 3 — bullet 1:

  <strong>Drought</strong> weakens vegetation cover, so bare soil bakes,
  organic matter drops, and dust storms become more likely in vulnerable
  regions.

--- Panel 3 — bullet 2:

  <strong>Erosion</strong> spikes when heavy rain hits bare slopes or compacted
  farm roads, sending sediment into streams and burying downstream habitat.

--- Panel 3 — bullet 3:

  <strong>Crop yields</strong> suffer when heat and water stress pile up during
  sensitive growth stages. That is why climate and agriculture show up
  together in news about food prices and insurance.

8 — ICE ALBEDO FEEDBACK
================================================================================

Section title:

  Ice albedo feedback is a positive loop

Section intro:

  A positive loop means that the loop is self reinforcing. 

Numbered steps (numbers 1–5 are fixed in layout; only the text after each is
editable on the site — edit the full line meaning here):

  Step 1 text:
  Sea ice and seasonal snow shrink as the region warms.

  Step 2 text:
  White ice and snow reflect a lot of sunlight back to space. Open water and
  bare ground are darker.

  Step 3 text:
  Albedo drops, meaning less reflection and more absorption of solar energy at
  the surface.

  Step 4 text:
  That extra absorbed energy becomes heat, which melts more ice and keeps the
  cycle going.

  Step 5 text:
  You end up with more warming and more melting until weather patterns,
  geography, or winter darkness finally slow the loop down.

Closing note paragraph:

Make sure you understand this it is really important. 

Second callout (Unit 1):

  <strong>Unit 1: Earth systems.</strong> Feedback loops tweak both the carbon
  cycle and Earth’s energy balance. Albedo shifts near the poles are one of the
  faster levers on how much sunlight the planet keeps.

9 — OBSERVED TRENDS (GRAPHS SECTION)

Section title:

  Observed trends

Section intro:

  The trends bellow show how concertation and temprature climb in relation to each other in the modern era. 

--- Subheading + intro — Why CO₂ rising?

  Why is CO₂ rising?

  Carbon dioxide mixes through the whole atmosphere, so a single monitoring
  station in Hawaii can still tell a global story when scientists account for
  seasons and averaging. The climb you is mainly created by human activities and not the volacano. 

Bullet 1:

  <strong>Fossil fuel burning.</strong> Coal, oil, and gas hold ancient carbon.
  Power plants, factories, ships, planes, and cars oxidize that carbon to CO₂
  fast compared with geologic time.

Bullet 2:

  <strong>Deforestation and land conversion.</strong> Forests store carbon in
  wood and soil. When we clear land and burn residue, some of that carbon returns
  to the air. Even without fire, a shorter rotation of crops usually stores less
  carbon than old growth.

Bullet 3:

  <strong>Industrial processes.</strong> Making cement releases CO₂ from
  limestone chemistry. Steel and chemicals often need high heat, historically
  from fossil fuels.

--- Subheading + intro — Why temperatures rising?

  Why are temperatures rising?

  Temperature bounces year to year because of El Niño, volcanoes, and other
  noise, which is why scientists emphasize trends over decades, not one hot
  afternoon.

Bullet 1:

  <strong>Greenhouse gases and the radiation budget.</strong> Earth absorbs
  sunlight and emits infrared. GHGs absorb some of that outgoing infrared and send
  energy back toward the surface. More GHGs tilt the balance toward warming until
  oceans and ice sheets adjust (which takes time).

Bullet 2:

  <strong>CO₂ is the long slow knob.</strong> It stays in the climate system for
  a long time compared with methane, so even if methane cuts matter a lot near
  term, CO₂ is still the main cumulative driver of the warming we have already
  baked in.

Tip 1 — title:
  Use public transportation
Tip 1 — description:
  If everyone uses public transportation then less carbon is produced beucase less people are using indidual vehicles. The carbon created per person in transport decreases and is shared. 

Tip 2 — title:
  Reduce meat consumption
Tip 2 — description:
  The production of meat in the agricultural industry creates a lot of carbon emissions. By switching to a vegitarian or vegan diet you will decrease the demand for meat which will decrease the amount of resources allocated to prodcucing meat. This will result in fewer emissions. 

Tip 3 — title:
  Switch to renewable energy
Tip 3 — description:
  Renewable energies prevent the burning of coal and other fossil fuels which releases carbon emissions. Instead by using technologies like solar pannels for your home you can decrease the amount of energy you use from combustion which will decrease the carbon emissions you are responsible for. 

Tip 4 — title:
  Reduce electricity usage
Tip 4 — description:
  You can try using less of your devices or appliances which will decrease your net electricity consumption. Another thing you can do is unplug your devices when they are not being using to avoid phantom load.

  Stores spesifically sell appliences that are more energy efficent.

  Reduce water waste
Tip 6 — description:
  It takes power to pump, heat, and treat water which can result in a larger footprint for you. So instead try using less water and reduce the amount that you waste. For example while brushing or washing the dishes turn the sink off when you are not using it.

  Compost food waste
